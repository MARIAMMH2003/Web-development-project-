Luxor Museum


Unveiling Ancient Treasures Anew


28th May 1975


The Luxor Museum, founded in 1975, stands as a beacon of Egypt's rich cultural
heritage. Nestled on the East Bank of the Nile, it boasts a stunning collection
of artifacts primarily from the Theban Necropolis and Luxor Temple. Showcasing a
fusion of ancient Egyptian and Greco-Roman influences, the museum offers a captivating
journey through millennia of history.

Visitors are greeted by a meticulously curated display featuring statues of
pharaohs, intricately carved reliefs, and exquisite artifacts unearthed from nearby
archaeological sites. Noteworthy highlights include the renowned granite colossus of
Amenhotep III and the beautifully preserved mummy of Ramses I.

The museum's design, with its airy galleries and expansive windows, provides
a serene backdrop for contemplation and exploration. Each artifact tells a story of
ancient rituals, royal power, and artistic mastery, offering glimpses into the lives
of the pharaohs and their subjects.

Beyond its permanent collection, the Luxor Museum hosts temporary exhibitions and
educational programs, engaging visitors of all ages with the wonders of ancient Egypt.
Whether admiring the delicate craftsmanship of a golden amulet or pondering the mysteries
of an inscribed stele, every visit to the Luxor Museum promises a deeper understanding of
this timeless civilization.

Famous Monuments



The Luxor Museum proudly houses the mummy of Ahmose I, the revered pharaoh of the early New Kingdom. Displayed with regal dignity, Ahmose's mummy offers a tangible connection to Egypt's illustrious past, embodying the splendor of ancient royalty for visitors to behold.

Tutankhamun's hunting chariot, an emblem of royal prestige, is showcased in the Luxor Museum. Crafted with exquisite detail, this artifact offers a glimpse into the opulent lifestyle of ancient Egyptian pharaohs, reflecting their passion for the hunt and their mastery of craftsmanship.

The Luxor Museum displays a remarkable black-and-gold wooden head of the cow goddess Mehit-Weret, found in Tutankhamun's tomb. This artifact exemplifies the ancient reverence for Hathor, showcasing exquisite craftsmanship and religious symbolism.

Ramesses I's mummy is among Luxor Museum's treasures, offering a poignant glimpse into ancient Egyptian royalty. Displayed with reverence, it reflects the intricate funerary practices and cultural significance of the time.

This greywacke statue of Thutmose III was found in the Karnak Cachette in 1904. With the legs below the knees missing, the statue measures at 90cm tall.Thutmose III created the largest empire Egypt had ever seen; no fewer than 17 campaigns were conducted and he conquered lands from the Niya Kingdom in northern Syria to the Fourth Cataract of the Nile in Nubia.
