The Coptic Museum


Preserving Heritage, Inspiring Culture


14th December 1908.


Embracing its rich heritage, the Coptic Museum in Cairo stands as a testament to the
enduring legacy of Egypt's Coptic Christian community. Established in 1908,
the museum is a sanctuary of cultural preservation and enlightenment. Within
its sacred walls, visitors are transported through centuries of Coptic history,
witnessing the evolution of art, faith, and tradition.

At the heart of the museum's collection lies a treasure trove of artifacts meticulously curated
to celebrate the enduring spirit of the Coptic people. From intricately woven textiles to exquisite icons,
each exhibit tells a story of resilience and devotion. Among its most revered artifacts is the famed
Fayyum portraits, exquisite portraits of ancient Coptic individuals capturing their timeless beauty and grace.

Yet, the Coptic Museum is more than a mere repository of relics—it is a vibrant hub of cultural exchange
and scholarship. Through innovative exhibitions and educational programs, the museum fosters a deeper understanding
of Coptic heritage and its significance in shaping Egypt's diverse tapestry of cultures.

As a guardian of Coptic identity and heritage, the museum plays a pivotal role in preserving the legacy
of Egypt's Coptic community. By safeguarding its rich cultural heritage, the museum ensures that the voices
and stories of the Coptic people endure for generations to come, inspiring future generations to embrace their
heritage with pride and reverence.

St. Barbara is among the most popular Christian saints in the East and West. She was locked in a tower by her pagan father, the rich Dioscuros from Nikomedia While he was travelling, Barbara converted to Christianity and eventually was beheaded by her own father.

Coptic gravestones often depict the deceased with their hands raised in prayer. This example was made for a woman who is dressed in a long garment. A traditional Roman mantle covers her head and shoulders and hangs down over her elbows.

This tunic was a garment commonly worn by males in Greco-Roman civilizations. At the top of this tunic, an arcade encloses figures of dancers and warriors. Below, two vertical panels with warriors and dancers alternate with roundels amid human busts.

This precious Bible casket of wood is covered with embossed silver sheets attached by nails. A rombic and floral pattern runs along all four sides. In the centre Virgin Mary with Jesus sitting on her lap are placed in between two Arabic lines.

Excavations at the Monastery of St. Jeremiah in Saqqara have yielded several niches that were pierced into the eastern walls of monks’ cells and served the monks for prayer and contemplation. These niches bear christological scenes showing the enthroned Jesus or Jesus Pantocrator. Two niches are decorated with the Virgin Mary nursing the infant Jesus
