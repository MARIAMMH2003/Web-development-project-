Greco-Roman Museum Of Alexandria


Echoes of Ancient Alexandria


26th April 1892


The Greco-Roman Museum of Alexandria, established in 1892, stands as a testament
to the city's rich cultural heritage. Housed in a neoclassical building, the museum
showcases artifacts spanning the Hellenistic and Roman periods of Egypt's history.
Visitors are greeted by a diverse collection of statues, sarcophagi, pottery, and
everyday objects, offering insight into the fusion of Greek and Egyptian cultures
during this era.

Among the museum's highlights are exquisite marble sculptures, intricate mosaics, and
intricately decorated pottery, reflecting the artistic prowess and cultural exchange that
flourished in ancient Alexandria. The museum also features artifacts from the underwater excavations
of the city's harbor, shedding light on its maritime past.

As visitors explore the galleries, they are transported back in time to an era of intellectual flourishing,
where Alexandria served as a vibrant center of learning, philosophy, and trade. From the grandeur of
royal portraits to the simplicity of household items, each artifact tells a story of the people who once
inhabited this cosmopolitan city.

The Greco-Roman Museum of Alexandria remains a vital link to the past, preserving and celebrating the legacy
of one of the ancient world's most influential cultural hubs.

Famous Monuments



The Statue of the god Hermanubis, a fusion deity combining the attributes of Hermes and Anubis, represents the cultural syncretism of Greco-Egyptian religion during the Ptolemaic period. This marble sculpture, dating back to the 2nd century AD, reflects the blending of Greek and Egyptian religious beliefs in ancient Alexandria, symbolizing the diverse cultural landscape of the city.

The Statue of the god Serapis, crafted during the Ptolemaic period in Egypt, embodies the fusion of Greek and Egyptian religious traditions. Serapis, a deity created by Ptolemy I, symbolizes the synthesis of Egyptian Osiris and Greek Zeus. This monumental statue reflects the cultural exchange and religious syncretism characteristic of Hellenistic Alexandria.
