"Nestled amidst the bustling streets of Cairo, the Museum of Islamic Arts stands as a timeless testament to Egypt's rich and vibrant Islamic heritage. Established in 1903, this architectural gem serves as a sanctuary of cultural preservation and enlightenment, inviting visitors on a captivating journey through the annals of Islamic civilization.
Stepping through its majestic gates, visitors are greeted by a treasure trove of artifacts that span centuries of Islamic history, art, and culture. From intricate textiles and dazzling ceramics to exquisite metalwork and breathtaking manuscripts, each exhibit offers a glimpse into the diverse and multifaceted world of Islamic creativity.
At the heart of the museum's collection lies a profound reverence for Islamic art and architecture. Intricately carved woodwork, mesmerizing geometric patterns, and majestic domes evoke the grandeur and splendor of Islamic civilization, while delicate calligraphy adorning ancient manuscripts speaks to the profound spiritual and intellectual legacy of the Islamic world.
Through immersive exhibitions and educational programs, the museum strives to foster a deeper understanding and appreciation for the rich tapestry of Islamic culture. From lectures and workshops to guided tours and interactive displays, visitors are offered a multitude of opportunities to explore and engage with the fascinating history and artistry of the Islamic world.

This kind of table was commonly called a dinner chair by some scholars of Islamic Art, whereas it was most probably used as a Quran holder in the mosques or for carrying the candlesticks during prayer time on both sides of the Miḥrāb. It could have also been used for holding personal accessories. The table takes the shape of a hexagonal prism decorated with vegetal, geometric, animal, and epigraphic motifs quoting Quranic and invocational phrases. A small double-leafed door opens up on one of its sides and is decorated with vegetal elements.

Ottoman ceramic products ranged from dishes, bowls, ewers and goblets, to perfume bottles, vases, pilgrimage flasks, and lamp suspension eggs. Ottoman ceramics reached high levels of precision and perfection. They became very popular on the European markets. The group from which this specimen derives is attributed to several Turkish cities such as Iznik, Kütahya, and Gallipoli (Canakkale). The artifacts in this group are decorated with under glaze polychromic motifs on white slip depicting tulips, cloves, and lancelet leaves. The vegetal motifs have played a vital role in the Ottoman Arts, and were frequently utilized on the Ottoman artistic products
